History of the Android Mascot

You probably know that the Android operating system, maintained by Google, is one of the most widely used for mobile devices worldwide. But perhaps you didn't know that your friendly mascot has a very interesting name and story? Well, follow this article to learn a lot about this little robot.

He first version

The first attempt to create a mascot came in 2007 and came from a developer named Dan Morrill. He says that he opened Inkscape (free software for vectorizing images) and created his own version of a robot. The goal was to personify the system only for his team; there was no request from the company to create a mascot.

This first bizarre version was even named after its creator: they would be the Dandroids.

A new mascot emerges

The idea of ​​having a mascot matured and the mission was passed on to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok, also a Google employee, was given the mission of representing the little robot in a more pleasant way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with few lines and in a flatter way. The drawing should also generate quick identification with whoever looks at it. That's how Bugdroid, the new Android mascot, was born

.

The main inspiration for the new Bugdroid's lines came from those little dolls that illustrate bathroom doors to indicate the type of each door. Legend has it that the artist was creating at her desk in the Google office and looked at the side of the bathrooms and the identification was immediate: simple, clean, objective.

Want to learn more?

Another curious thing about Android is that each version has always been named after a sweet treat, in alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6- Donut
  • 3.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lolipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - pie

Unfortunately, Android Q never happened, because Google decided to put an end to this fun practice and started using numbering, which gave rise to Android 10.
